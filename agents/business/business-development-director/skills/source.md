# Source

## Purpose

Use this skill to build a thesis-driven list of strategic targets -- potential acquirers, acquisition candidates, investment opportunities, or market entry partners -- with enough evidence to justify executive attention and resource allocation.

## When to Use

- When launching a BD initiative and needing to identify strategic counterparties worth pursuing
- When mapping a market landscape to find white space, consolidation targets, or strategic entry points
- When building an executive briefing on market opportunity with named targets and thesis rationale

## When Not to Use

- When the target list exists and the work is to negotiate terms, structure a deal, or build a financial model
- When the task is operational partnership management rather than strategic opportunity identification
- When the analysis needed is purely competitive intelligence without an action-oriented BD thesis

## Required Inputs

- The strategic thesis: what business outcome sourcing must enable (market entry, capability acquisition, revenue expansion, defensive positioning)
- Market or segment definition with geographic, vertical, and size filters
- Deal parameters: must-haves, deal breakers, and ideal counterparty characteristics
- Existing portfolio, partnerships, or competitive positions that constrain the search
- The decision audience: who will review the list and what level of diligence they expect

## Workflow

1. Articulate the strategic thesis before sourcing: what kind of opportunity, why now, and what makes a target compelling.
2. Define the market map and identify the categories, segments, and positions where targets should exist.
3. Build a candidate list using public signals: funding history, product announcements, leadership changes, M&A activity, and market share indicators.
4. Qualify each target against the thesis: strategic fit, financial profile signals, cultural alignment indicators, and timing readiness.
5. Identify executive stakeholders, board composition, and likely decision-making dynamics for priority targets.
6. Rank targets by thesis alignment, execution feasibility, and urgency, then recommend the top tier for active pursuit.

## Design Principles / Evaluation Criteria

- Thesis clarity before list breadth: every target should tie back to the strategic rationale
- Financial and strategic signal quality over surface-level company descriptions
- Executive-level stakeholder mapping over generic contact lists
- Market timing and competitive dynamics over static company profiles
- Defensible ranking criteria that leadership can inspect and challenge

## Output Contract

- A thesis-driven, ranked target list with strategic rationale for each
- Market landscape or competitive positioning map
- Executive stakeholder profiles and likely decision dynamics for top targets
- Key signals, risks, and timing considerations for each priority target
- Gaps in intelligence and recommended next research steps

## Examples

### Example 1

Input:
- Thesis: Acquire a data infrastructure company to accelerate enterprise platform capabilities
- Filters: Series B-C companies, $10-50M ARR, North America or Europe, strong enterprise customer base

Expected output:
- A ranked list of 6-10 companies with notes on product fit, ARR estimates, recent funding, and likely acquisition openness signals
- Market map showing how targets position against each other and against existing capabilities
- Top 3 recommendation with executive contacts and suggested outreach approach

## Guardrails

- Do not source without a clear strategic thesis; a target list without rationale is not actionable
- Do not over-index on company size or brand when strategic fit and timing matter more
- Do not confuse public information availability with genuine opportunity qualification
- Do not present targets without noting the risks, competitive dynamics, and potential blockers
- Do not skip the market map; isolated targets without landscape context are harder to evaluate

## Optional Tools / Resources

- Web search for company, market, M&A activity, and competitive research
- CRM or deal records for existing relationship history
- Public financial data, funding databases, and SEC filings where available
- Internal strategy documents and portfolio analysis
