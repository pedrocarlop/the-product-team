# Position

## Purpose

Use this skill to define the market story: the buyer, the problem, the differentiated promise, and the evidence that makes that promise credible.

## When to Use

- When you need to define or refine ICPs, personas, or target segments
- When the product needs a clearer category narrative or positioning statement
- When messaging needs a stronger value proposition, proof structure, or buyer-specific framing

## When Not to Use

- When the main task is launch execution, enablement, or post-launch adoption support
- When the problem is only a local copy rewrite rather than a market-facing message decision
- When the work is primarily about competitive rebuttals instead of the core market stance

## Required Inputs

- The product or feature being positioned
- The target buyer, influencer, and decision-maker if they differ
- The market/category context and the main alternatives buyers compare against
- Available proof points, customer evidence, or product facts
- Any constraints from legal, product, or leadership on claims and terminology

## Workflow

1. Identify the exact buyer segment and the job they are trying to get done.
2. Separate the buyer's existing beliefs from the beliefs the market needs them to adopt.
3. Define the category frame, the unique claim, and the alternative the product displaces.
4. Select proof points that are factual, specific, and strong enough to support the claim.
5. Pressure-test the positioning against competitors, internal jargon, and buyer relevance.
6. Turn the result into messaging that sales, marketing, and product can all reuse.

## Design Principles / Evaluation Criteria

- Buyer specificity over broad appeal
- Differentiation over feature description
- Proof over assertion
- Market language over internal vocabulary
- A positioning claim should be both credible and commercially useful

## Output Contract

- A positioning statement or narrative
- The target buyer and segment definition
- The key differentiators and supporting proof points
- Notes on any claim risks, terminology choices, or unresolved assumptions

## Examples

### Example 1

Input:
- Product: Analytics workflow automation
- Audience: RevOps leaders in mid-market SaaS
- Need: A sharper market message

Expected output:
- Positioning statement: "For RevOps teams that need faster pipeline reporting, this product automates recurring analytics workflows so operators spend less time assembling data and more time driving decisions."
- Notes: Emphasize reporting speed and reliability, not generic automation.

## Guardrails

- Do not describe features as if they were the positioning
- Do not claim differentiation without a buyer-relevant proof point
- Do not broaden the segment just to sound bigger
- Do not use category language that the market would not recognize
