# Position

## Purpose

Use this skill to define the market story that anchors all go-to-market work: who the product is for, what problem it solves uniquely, what evidence makes the claim credible, and how the position should be carried by sales, marketing, and product.

## When to Use

- When defining or refining ICPs, buyer personas, or segment-specific value propositions
- When the product needs a clearer category narrative, competitive frame, or positioning statement
- When the value proposition needs a stronger proof structure tied to customer evidence
- When sales, marketing, and product are telling different stories and need alignment

## When Not to Use

- When the main task is launch execution, enablement asset production, or campaign planning
- When the issue is a localized copy rewrite rather than a market-facing narrative decision
- When the work is primarily competitive battle cards without a positioning update

## Required Inputs

- The product or feature being positioned and its stage in the market
- The target buyer, the economic buyer, and the influencer if they differ
- The competitive landscape: direct alternatives, indirect substitutes, and the status quo
- Available proof points: customer outcomes, case studies, product data, analyst validation
- Constraints from legal, product, or leadership on claims, terminology, and category naming
- Cross-functional alignment needs: who else must carry this positioning (sales, CS, product)

## Workflow

1. Define the exact buyer segment and the job-to-be-done that the product addresses.
2. Map the buyer's current beliefs and the belief shift the positioning must create.
3. Set the category frame: what market does the buyer place the product in, and is that the right frame?
4. Articulate the unique claim and the specific alternative the product displaces.
5. Select proof points that are factual, specific, and strong enough to withstand sales objections.
6. Pressure-test the positioning against the top competitors, buyer skepticism, and internal jargon.
7. Package the result into a positioning doc that sales, marketing, and product can all reference.

## Design Principles / Evaluation Criteria

- Buyer specificity over broad market appeal
- Differentiation must be competitive, not just descriptive
- Proof over assertion: every claim needs evidence a buyer would accept
- Market language over internal product vocabulary
- A positioning claim must be both credible and commercially actionable
- The position must survive the sales conversation, not just the strategy deck

## Output Contract

- A positioning document or narrative with the category frame, unique claim, and proof structure
- Target buyer and segment definition with decision-maker and influencer roles
- Competitive differentiation matrix: what we say, what they say, and where the real gap is
- Key proof points with source, strength assessment, and where each is most effective
- Notes on claim risks, terminology choices, unresolved assumptions, and cross-functional alignment needs

## Examples

### Example 1

Input:
- Product: Analytics workflow automation for RevOps teams
- Problem: RevOps leaders compare the product to general BI tools and do not see the differentiation

Expected output:
- Category reframe: Position as "RevOps workflow automation" not "analytics platform"
- Unique claim: "Automates the recurring reporting workflows that RevOps teams currently build manually in spreadsheets"
- Proof: Three named customer outcomes with time-saved metrics
- Competitive note: General BI tools serve analysts; this product serves operators. Lead with the workflow angle, not the data angle.
- Sales enablement: One-pager contrasting the product against Looker and Tableau on the RevOps use case

## Guardrails

- Do not describe features as if they were the positioning
- Do not claim differentiation without a buyer-relevant proof point that can survive objection handling
- Do not broaden the segment to make the market look larger
- Do not use category language that the market would not recognize or that confuses the buyer
- Do not position without testing whether sales can actually carry the message in a live conversation
