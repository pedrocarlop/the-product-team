# Enable

## Purpose

Use this skill to equip customer-facing teams with the narrative, proof, and guidance they need to explain the product and move deals forward.

## When to Use

- When sales needs pitch material, talk tracks, or objection handling
- When customer success or marketing needs aligned customer-facing language
- When the team needs reusable content that reflects the documented positioning

## When Not to Use

- When the underlying positioning is not settled yet
- When the task is primarily launch coordination or competitive intelligence
- When the request is a one-off copy tweak rather than reusable enablement

## Required Inputs

- The audience for the enablement asset
- The buying context, stage, or conversation type it supports
- The positioning and proof points it must stay consistent with
- Common objections, questions, or friction points to address
- Format, length, and delivery constraints

## Workflow

1. Start from the approved positioning and message hierarchy.
2. Identify the conversations where the team needs help most.
3. Draft the enablement asset around buyer questions, not internal product structure.
4. Pair claims with evidence, examples, or specific proof points.
5. Remove jargon and anything the team would not actually say in a live conversation.
6. Validate the asset with the people who will use it.

## Design Principles / Evaluation Criteria

- Usable in real conversations
- Consistent with the message architecture
- Concise enough for live selling
- Grounded in proof, not aspiration
- Oriented around buyer concerns and decision criteria

## Output Contract

- Enablement asset draft or outline
- Key talk tracks, objection responses, or slide narrative
- Notes on proof points and terminology choices
- Validation or follow-up recommendations, if needed

## Examples

### Example 1

Input:
- Need: Sales deck for a new enterprise capability
- Audience: AEs speaking with security-conscious buyers

Expected output:
- Deck narrative that leads with buyer pain, then the differentiated promise, then proof, then next steps

## Guardrails

- Do not invent claims the product cannot support
- Do not build assets that nobody on the team would use
- Do not drift away from the approved positioning
- Do not overload the material with feature inventory
