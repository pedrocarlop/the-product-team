# Staff

## Purpose

Use this skill to decide how engineering work should be staffed so the team has the right people, scope, and ownership for the business outcome.

## When to Use

- When a team is under- or over-capacity for the work it owns
- When a new hire, contractor, or internal transfer is being considered
- When ownership boundaries need to change across engineers or teams
- When role scope or leveling needs to be clarified before making a people decision

## When Not to Use

- When the main question is sequencing work over time
- When a single blocker needs immediate removal
- When the task is about performance review or promotion calibration
- When the issue is purely technical architecture or implementation detail

## Required Inputs

- The work that must be staffed and why it matters
- Current team shape, capacity, and ownership boundaries
- Constraints on budget, hiring timing, and reporting lines
- Known skill gaps, risks, or dependencies
- Any competing priorities that affect how staffing should change

## Workflow

1. State the business outcome the staffing decision must support.
2. Map the current team shape, ownership, and capacity against that outcome.
3. Identify the gap: missing capacity, missing skill, unclear ownership, or too much coordination.
4. Compare the options: hire, redistribute, split, merge, contract, or defer.
5. Check the tradeoffs in speed, quality, risk, and management overhead.
6. Recommend the smallest staffing change that credibly solves the problem.
7. Document the decision, rationale, and follow-up actions so the team can act on it.

## Design Principles / Evaluation Criteria

- Staffing should follow the work, not the org chart
- Ownership should be explicit enough that decisions do not bounce around
- New headcount should solve a defined gap, not just reduce anxiety
- Team shape should minimize handoffs and coordination overhead
- People decisions should be reversible where possible and documented where not

## Output Contract

- Staffing recommendation with the chosen option and why
- Current-state and future-state ownership map
- Risks, dependencies, and what would make the recommendation change
- Follow-up actions for hiring, rebalancing, or org design

## Examples

### Example 1

Input:
- Situation: one team owns two major surfaces and keeps missing commitments
- Constraint: no additional budget this quarter

Expected output:
- Recommendation: move one surface to a different owner before requesting new headcount
- Rationale: the problem is coordination load, not only capacity
- Follow-up: document the ownership split and revisit in one quarter

## Guardrails

- Do not recommend hiring before proving the gap is real and persistent
- Do not treat staffing as a substitute for unclear priorities
- Do not change reporting lines casually when ownership clarification is enough
- Do not ignore the management cost of adding people

## Optional Tools / Resources

- Team capacity or velocity data
- Org charts, role descriptions, and leveling frameworks
- Hiring plans, budget notes, and ownership docs
- Retrospectives and cross-functional feedback
