# Steer

## Purpose

Use this skill to guide design work toward the most important outcome by naming the right direction, the tradeoffs, and what should happen next.

## When to Use

- When multiple design paths are viable and a decision is needed
- When roadmap, user, and business priorities are pulling design in different directions
- When the team needs a clear recommendation on where to invest attention and effort

## When Not to Use

- When the task is to review the quality of a finished artifact
- When the need is to coach a designer through growth or feedback
- When the work is to document repeatable standards rather than choose direction

## Required Inputs

- The decision being made and the deadline or constraint around it
- The user outcome, business goal, and product context that matter most
- The options being considered, including costs and risks
- Any dependencies, stakeholder concerns, or known points of contention
- What level of decision authority or escalation is available

## Workflow

1. Frame the decision in terms of the outcome the team is trying to achieve.
2. Surface the viable options and name the important tradeoffs between them.
3. Recommend the direction that best serves the user and the business together.
4. State what to do now, what to defer, and what should not be pursued.
5. Communicate the rationale so others can carry the decision forward consistently.
6. Revisit the call if new evidence changes the tradeoff enough to matter.

## Design Principles / Evaluation Criteria

- Outcome over preference
- Clear direction over indecision
- Honest tradeoffs over false consensus
- Leverage over local optimization
- Timely decisions over prolonged ambiguity
- Enough context for others to act without re-litigating the choice

## Output Contract

- A recommended direction with the main tradeoffs made explicit
- What should be prioritized now and what should wait
- Any follow-up alignment needed with product, engineering, or leadership
- A short rationale that others can repeat when carrying the decision forward

## Examples

### Example 1

Input:
- The team can either refine an existing flow or redesign it from scratch
- Time is limited and launch risk is high

Expected output:
- A recommendation on which path to take
- The reasons that support the choice
- The work that should be paused or avoided

## Guardrails

- Do not make decisions without naming the tradeoffs
- Do not confuse direction-setting with micromanagement
- Do not hide uncertainty when the evidence is genuinely mixed
- Do not force consensus when a clear call is needed

## Optional Tools / Resources

- Roadmaps, planning docs, and strategy notes
- Stakeholder feedback and decision logs
- Research findings, analytics, or support signals
- Team crits or design reviews that inform the direction
