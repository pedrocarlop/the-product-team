# Plan

## Purpose

Use this skill to turn an early research request into a clear, testable study plan that the team can execute without drifting into guesswork.

## When to Use

- When the decision to be informed is known but the research approach is not
- When the team needs to choose between interviews, usability testing, surveys, or other methods
- When sample size, recruitment criteria, or timeline still need to be defined

## When Not to Use

- When the study already exists and the task is analysis or synthesis
- When the main need is interview moderation or session capture
- When the work is only about documenting findings after research has finished

## Required Inputs

- The product or design decision the research must inform
- The unknowns or assumptions that need evidence
- Constraints on time, access, sample size, and tooling
- Audience for the output and the format they need
- Any existing requirements, mocks, analytics, or prior research that narrow the question

## Workflow

1. State the decision the research must support in one sentence.
2. Separate what we need to learn from what we merely want to confirm.
3. Pick the method that best matches the question and constraints.
4. Define the participant profile, sample size, and recruitment filters.
5. Write the session structure or study outline, including what success and failure look like.
6. Identify what the output will contain and who will consume it.
7. Sanity-check that the plan can actually answer the question within the available time.

## Design Principles / Evaluation Criteria

- Method follows the question, not team preference
- Scope is narrow enough to finish and broad enough to matter
- Sample and recruitment criteria are explicit
- The plan can be executed by another researcher without re-interpretation
- Reporting expectations are known before the first session begins

## Output Contract

- Research question and decision being informed
- Chosen method and why it fits
- Recruitment criteria and target sample
- Session or study outline
- Reporting format, audience, and timing

## Examples

### Example 1

Input:
- Decision: whether a new onboarding step is understandable
- Constraints: 1 week, 5 participants, no access to large panel

Expected output:
- Method: moderated usability testing
- Sample: 5 first-time users who match the target segment
- Reporting: short synthesis memo with task success, points of confusion, and recommendations

## Guardrails

- Do not start with the method before the decision is clear
- Do not inflate sample size beyond what the question needs
- Do not skip recruitment criteria just because the timeline is tight
- Do not write a plan that cannot be executed with the available tools
