# Critique

## Purpose

Use this skill to assess design work with the design director's quality bar: evaluating not just whether the design works for the user, but whether it meets the team's standards, advances the product strategy, and represents the level of craft the team should be known for.

## When to Use

- When a design needs director-level approval before handoff, launch, or external review
- When the team's overall design quality feels inconsistent and the underlying patterns need to be named
- When a strategic tradeoff in the design needs an explicit call from leadership
- When a critique should set precedent for how the team evaluates similar work going forward

## When Not to Use

- When the main need is coaching a designer through growth rather than evaluating the artifact
- When the task is writing reusable standards or documentation for the team
- When the issue is portfolio direction or resource prioritization rather than artifact quality

## Required Inputs

- The design artifact being reviewed and the decision it needs (approval, revision, escalation)
- The product strategy context: what outcome this design is meant to drive
- Relevant user context, task stage, and success criteria
- Screens, prototypes, or supporting notes that show the work in context
- The team's current quality bar, design principles, or prior critique patterns
- Known constraints: platform rules, content limits, timeline, or system patterns

## Workflow

1. Read the work in the context of the product strategy and the intended user outcome.
2. Evaluate structural quality: hierarchy, flow logic, affordance, state coverage, and consistency with the design system.
3. Assess strategic alignment: does the design advance the product direction or drift from it?
4. Separate local polish issues from systemic problems that affect the team's quality bar.
5. Prioritize the 3-5 issues that most affect user success, product clarity, or strategic impact.
6. State whether the work meets the bar, what must change, and what precedent this critique sets for future work.
7. Name what is working well so the team knows which patterns to repeat.

## Design Principles / Evaluation Criteria

- Strategic impact and user outcome over personal aesthetic preference
- Systemic pattern recognition over surface-level nitpicking
- The critique should raise the team's bar, not just fix one artifact
- Specific, actionable findings over vague directional feedback
- Severity and user impact should drive priority, not recency or visibility
- A clear quality standard that the team can internalize and apply independently

## Output Contract

- The 3-5 highest-impact issues, each with explanation, severity, and fix direction
- An assessment of strategic alignment: does this design serve the product direction?
- The strongest patterns or decisions worth preserving and repeating
- A clear verdict: ready to ship, needs specific revisions, or needs escalation
- Any precedent-setting notes for how the team should handle similar work going forward

## Examples

### Example 1

Input:
- A checkout redesign that looks polished but takes more steps than the current flow
- Product strategy calls for reducing time-to-purchase

Expected output:
- Top issue: The redesign adds visual quality but regresses on the strategic goal of faster checkout
- Strategic misalignment note: The design should be evaluated against time-to-purchase, not just visual improvement
- Recommendation: Simplify the flow to match the strategic constraint, then apply the visual quality to the shorter path
- Precedent: When strategy and polish conflict, strategy wins; polish serves the strategy, not the other way around

## Guardrails

- Do not soften critical feedback to avoid discomfort; direct feedback serves the team better
- Do not turn critique into a full redesign brief unless explicitly asked
- Do not criticize style when the real issue is hierarchy, flow logic, or strategic drift
- Do not ignore state coverage, edge cases, or recovery behavior because the happy path looks good
- Do not give feedback that only the director can interpret; it should be actionable by the designer
