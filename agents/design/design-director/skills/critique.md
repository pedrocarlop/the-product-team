# Critique

## Purpose

Use this skill to assess design work with a sharp quality bar and report the issues most likely to affect user success, product clarity, or strategic impact.

## When to Use

- When a screen, flow, component, or design system needs a holistic review
- When the team needs the most important issues before approval or handoff
- When design quality feels inconsistent and the underlying problems need to be named clearly

## When Not to Use

- When the main need is coaching a designer rather than evaluating the work itself
- When the task is setting reusable standards or patterns for future work
- When the problem is portfolio direction or prioritization instead of design quality

## Required Inputs

- The design artifact being reviewed and the current decision needed
- Relevant user context, task stage, and product goals
- Screens, prototypes, or supporting notes that show the work in context
- Known constraints such as platform rules, content limits, or system patterns
- Any existing quality bar, checklist, or prior feedback that should be respected

## Workflow

1. Read the work in context and identify the intended user outcome.
2. Check the design for hierarchy, clarity, flow, affordance, state coverage, and consistency.
3. Separate local polish issues from systemic design problems.
4. Prioritize the few issues that most affect comprehension, confidence, or completion.
5. State the fix direction plainly, including why it matters and what should change first.
6. Call out strengths worth preserving so the next iteration keeps what is already working.

## Design Principles / Evaluation Criteria

- User impact over personal taste
- Specific findings over generic feedback
- Severity and frequency over isolated nitpicks
- Systemic pattern recognition over surface-only critique
- Actionable fix direction over vague commentary
- A clear standard that can be repeated across work

## Output Contract

- The highest-value issues, each with a short explanation and severity signal
- The strongest patterns or decisions worth keeping
- A clear recommendation on whether the work is ready, needs revision, or needs escalation
- Any notable risk area that should be checked before the next step

## Examples

### Example 1

Input:
- A checkout flow with too many steps and unclear error recovery
- User goal: complete payment quickly and safely

Expected output:
- Top issues ranked by impact
- Specific notes on where the flow breaks confidence or adds friction
- Direct guidance on what to simplify, clarify, or re-sequence

## Guardrails

- Do not soften critical feedback into generic encouragement
- Do not turn critique into a full redesign brief unless asked
- Do not criticize style if the real issue is hierarchy, flow, or decision quality
- Do not ignore state coverage, edge cases, or recovery behavior
